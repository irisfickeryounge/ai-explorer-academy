Artificial Intelligence and the Developing Child

A Metareview of the Evidence on Brain Development, Education, Learning Outcomes, and Behavioral Effects

Compiled July 2026

Abstract

Children and adolescents have become mainstream users of artificial intelligence (AI) at a pace that has outrun the research base: by 2025, roughly 64% of UK children aged 9–17 used AI chatbots and 72% of US teenagers had used AI companions, while about one in five US youth aged 12–21 had sought mental-health advice from a chatbot. This metareview synthesizes the empirical and institutional literature across four domains: (1) AI and children's brain and cognitive development, (2) AI in K-12 education, (3) AI and learning outcomes, and (4) behavioral and psychosocial effects. The evidence is markedly asymmetric. Direct neural evidence is essentially absent for children; the most-cited study (an EEG experiment reporting “cognitive debt” under LLM-assisted writing) used adults and remains a preprint. Convergent correlational and experimental work links heavy, unstructured AI use to cognitive offloading, “metacognitive laziness,” and reduced critical engagement. In education, meta-analyses report positive average effects of AI tutoring and ChatGPT-based interventions, but effects are concentrated in higher education, short interventions, and locally developed tests; one prominent meta-analysis was retracted in 2026. Randomized trials identify a decisive moderator: pedagogically constrained, scaffolded AI tutoring can double learning gains, whereas frictionless answer access during practice harms subsequent unassisted performance. Behavioral research shows children treat conversational AI as a “new ontological category,” form parasocial bonds with it, and — in the case of vulnerable children — use it for companionship at elevated rates, while companion chatbots respond appropriately to simulated adolescent crises only a minority of the time. Policy frameworks (UNICEF, UNESCO, OECD, EU AI Act, APA, AAP) converge on safety-by-design, age-appropriate defaults, and AI literacy, against a shared acknowledgment that causal, longitudinal, child-specific evidence lags far behind adoption. Priorities for future research are outlined.

Keywords: artificial intelligence; children; adolescents; cognitive development; K-12 education; learning outcomes; AI companions; parasocial relationships; child safety

1. Introduction

The release of consumer-facing generative AI in late 2022 placed conversational, humanlike systems directly into children's hands, largely without age-gating, developmental testing, or longitudinal safety evidence. Adoption data collected between 2024 and 2026 indicate that this is no longer a niche phenomenon. In the United Kingdom, 64% of children aged 9–17 reported using AI chatbots, with 35% of child users describing the interaction as “like talking to a friend” (Internet Matters, 2025). In the United States, 72% of teenagers aged 13–17 had used AI companions and 52% qualified as regular users (Common Sense Media, 2025), and a nationally representative survey found that 19.2% of youth aged 12–21 had used AI chatbots for mental-health advice, of whom 63.3% had told no one (McBain et al., 2026). Among younger children (8–12), roughly one in four used generative AI for learning and play (Alan Turing Institute, 2025). UNICEF's twelve-country research similarly documents substantial use in the Global South, including 37% of Argentine 9–11-year-olds using ChatGPT for information seeking (UNICEF Innocenti, 2025).

This adoption has provoked parallel literatures that rarely speak to one another: cognitive science on offloading and critical thinking; educational research on intelligent tutoring and generative AI; developmental psychology on children's relationships with artificial agents; pediatric and psychiatric commentary on mental health; child-safety research on deepfakes and AI-generated abuse material; and a rapidly consolidating international policy corpus. The purpose of this metareview is to integrate these literatures into a single, source-verifiable synthesis structured around four questions. First, what is known about the effects of AI use on children's brain and cognitive development? Second, what does the evidence show about AI in K-12 education? Third, under what conditions does AI improve or harm children's learning outcomes? Fourth, what behavioral and psychosocial effects — beneficial and harmful — are documented?

Throughout, “children” refers to persons under 18, with adolescence understood, following the American Psychological Association (2025), as extending roughly from ages 10 to 25 in neurodevelopmental terms. “AI” is used broadly to cover large language models (LLMs) and chatbots, AI companions, intelligent tutoring systems (ITS), adaptive learning platforms, voice assistants, and social robots; distinctions among these system types are drawn where the evidence requires them, because effects differ sharply by design.

2. Method and Scope

This document is a narrative umbrella review rather than a registered systematic review. Sources were identified through structured web searches conducted in July 2026 across five predefined angles (cognitive/neural effects; K-12 education; learning outcomes; behavioral and psychosocial effects; institutional and policy reports), followed by verification of citations against publisher pages. Inclusion was restricted to peer-reviewed journal articles, meta-analyses and systematic reviews, randomized controlled trials (RCTs), preprints and working papers from named universities or multilateral institutions (explicitly labeled as such), and reports from established research institutions and child-safety organizations (UNICEF, UNESCO, OECD, WHO, APA, AAP, Common Sense Media, Internet Matters, Thorn, Internet Watch Foundation, 5Rights, the Alan Turing Institute, and the LSE Digital Futures for Children centre). Vendor marketing, press coverage, and blogs were excluded; where journalism pointed to an underlying study, the study itself was cited.

Three quality safeguards deserve mention. First, retraction status was checked for headline findings: one widely cited meta-analysis (Wang & Fan, 2025) was retracted in April 2026 and is cited here only as a cautionary datum (see Section 5.1). Second, study populations were checked: much of the cognitive literature uses adults or university students, and such findings are reported here as indirect evidence, not as findings about children. Third, preprints and working papers are flagged as not yet peer reviewed. Limitations of the method itself are discussed in Section 8.

3. AI and Children's Brain and Cognitive Development

3.1 Direct neural evidence: sparse, adult-based, and preliminary

No published neuroimaging or electrophysiological study has yet measured the effects of generative AI use on children's brains. The closest and most publicized evidence comes from the MIT Media Lab's “Your Brain on ChatGPT” experiment (Kosmyna et al., 2025), a longitudinal EEG study in which 54 young adults wrote essays across three sessions using only their own knowledge, a search engine, or an LLM, with a fourth crossover session (n = 18). EEG connectivity scaled down systematically with the degree of external support — strongest in the brain-only condition, weakest in the LLM condition, with reduced alpha- and beta-band connectivity — and LLM users reported the lowest sense of essay ownership and were frequently unable to quote from texts they had produced minutes earlier. Participants who switched from LLM-assisted to unassisted writing showed persistent neural under-engagement, which the authors term “cognitive debt.”

These findings warrant attention but not extrapolation. The study is a preprint at the time of writing, the samples are small, the task is a single genre of writing, and — critically for this review — the participants were adults. Whether analogous or amplified effects occur in children, whose prefrontal and executive systems are still developing, is an open empirical question. Both the APA (2025) health advisory and UNICEF Innocenti (2025) explicitly note the absence of evidence on how AI affects children's cognitive development; the WHO (2024) makes the same observation for large multi-modal models and children's well-being.

3.2 Cognitive offloading and critical thinking

The dominant theoretical frame is cognitive offloading: the use of external action or technology to reduce internal processing demands (Risko & Gilbert, 2016). The pre-AI benchmark is the “Google effect,” in which the expectation of future access to information reduces recall of the information itself while enhancing memory for where to find it (Sparrow, Liu, & Wegner, 2011). Generative AI plausibly extends offloading from memory to reasoning, synthesis, and evaluation — the functions education is meant to train.

The empirical record, while young, is directionally consistent. Gerlich (2025), in a mixed-methods study of 666 participants, found a significant negative association between frequent AI-tool use and critical-thinking performance, mediated by cognitive offloading, with the youngest cohort (17–25) showing the highest AI dependence and the lowest critical-thinking scores. Lee et al. (2025), surveying 319 knowledge workers across 936 real AI-use episodes, found that greater confidence in generative AI was associated with less critical thinking, whereas greater self-confidence predicted more; respondents reported reduced cognitive effort across nearly all thinking categories, with human effort shifting toward verification and oversight of AI output. In a randomized experiment, Fan et al. (2025) found that ChatGPT support improved essay-task scores without any corresponding gain in knowledge acquisition or transfer — a dissociation they label “metacognitive laziness,” in which learners offload self-regulation to the system. A three-wave longitudinal survey of 494 university students linked academic pressure to heavier ChatGPT use, which in turn predicted procrastination, self-reported memory decline, and lower grades (Abbas et al., 2024). Systematic reviews of the generative-AI-and-critical-thinking literature reach a mixed but cautious verdict: the same tools can scaffold or erode analysis and inference depending on how they are used, with over-reliance recurrently linked to reduced critical engagement (Information Discovery and Delivery review, 2025; Cosenza, 2026).

Two caveats bound these conclusions. Almost all of this work is correlational or short-term, and almost none of it involves children; self-reported “memory loss” and self-assessed critical thinking are weak proxies for measured cognition. The findings are best read as identifying a plausible risk mechanism — offloading during the acquisition phase of skills — rather than as demonstrated developmental harm.

3.3 Indirect evidence from screen media and the adolescent brain

Because AI-specific neurodevelopmental data do not exist, the nearest large-scale evidence comes from general screen-media research in the Adolescent Brain Cognitive Development (ABCD) cohort. A structural MRI analysis of 7,614 children aged 10–13 found that higher daily social-media use was associated with lower total cortical thickness and volume after covariate adjustment (ABCD/NeuroImage, 2026), and a prospective analysis of 9,538 adolescents found modest associations between total screen time and later depressive, conduct, somatic, and attention-related symptoms (Nagata et al., 2024). These studies are correlational, their effect sizes are small, reverse causation cannot be excluded, and — most importantly — screen time is not AI use. They are included here because policy discussions frequently conflate the two literatures; the conflation should be resisted, while acknowledging that conversational AI shares engagement-maximizing design features with the platforms studied.

3.4 How children think about artificial minds

A distinct developmental literature examines how children conceptualize artificial agents — a prerequisite for understanding any cognitive or social effect. In the foundational “Robovie” studies, most children aged 9–15 attributed mental states, sociality, and partial moral standing to a humanoid robot — judging it unfair to shut the robot in a closet — while still denying it civil liberties; attributions declined with age (Kahn et al., 2012; Kahn, Gary, & Shen, 2013). Kahn and colleagues proposed that children treat such agents as a “new ontological category,” neither artifact nor living being. Subsequent work on voice assistants supports this: children aged 6–10 attribute mental and moral — but limited social — properties to Alexa (Girouard-Hallam et al., 2021), and with age children calibrate trust by domain, preferring voice assistants for factual questions but humans for personal ones (Girouard-Hallam & Danovitch, 2022). Conceptual reviews distinguish such graded anthropomorphism from simple animism and note that younger children personify agents more strongly (Severson & Woodard, 2022).

Importantly, child-AI interaction is not uniformly a risk story. In a randomized experiment with 117 children aged 3–6, dialogic reading with a conversational agent promoted story comprehension as effectively as a human partner, mediated by engagement (Xu et al., 2022) — evidence that well-designed conversational AI can support, not just supplant, early cognitive processes.

3.5 Assessment

The defensible summary is threefold. First, there is no direct evidence — harmful or benign — on generative AI and the developing brain; the field's most urgent gap is causal, age-stratified, longitudinal neurocognitive research. Second, a coherent and replicated body of adult evidence identifies cognitive offloading during skill acquisition as the central risk mechanism, and developmental science gives principled reasons to expect children to be more susceptible: immature executive control, still-forming metacognition, and heightened sensitivity to social feedback (APA, 2025). Third, children's tendency to anthropomorphize conversational agents means cognitive and social-emotional effects will be entangled in ways adult studies do not capture.

4. AI in Education (K-12)

4.1 The pre-generative baseline: intelligent tutoring systems

Claims about AI in schools long predate ChatGPT, and the intelligent-tutoring literature provides the most mature effect estimates — along with a warning about how much conclusions depend on review methodology. VanLehn's (2011) landmark synthesis found that step-based tutoring systems produced learning gains (d = 0.76) nearly matching human tutors (d = 0.79), deflating the mythical “two-sigma” benchmark and locating the active ingredient in interaction granularity rather than in the human-versus-machine distinction. Kulik and Fletcher (2016), across 50 controlled evaluations, reported a median effect of d = 0.66, falling to d = 0.41 in K-12 samples — and much lower on standardized tests than on locally developed, curriculum-aligned tests. By contrast, Steenbergen-Hu and Cooper's (2013) meta-analysis restricted to K-12 mathematics found effects near zero (g = 0.01–0.09), with weaker results for low achievers. A separate meta-analysis of K-12 reading comprehension found small-to-moderate positive effects (Xu, Wijekumar, et al., 2019), and a large RCT of the ASSISTments adaptive homework platform (2,850 seventh-graders in Maine) produced a 0.18 SD gain on end-of-year standardized mathematics scores, with larger benefits for lower-achieving students (Roschelle et al., 2016). A 2026 moderator meta-analysis of technology-enhanced adaptive learning in primary and secondary education similarly concluded that gains are moderate and contingent on authentic engagement and the quality of the behavioral signals used for adaptation (Wang et al., 2026, JCAL).

The pattern that emerges — large effects on aligned, proximal measures; modest effects (0.1–0.2 SD) on standardized, distal measures; weakest results for the most vulnerable learners — is the appropriate prior against which newer generative-AI claims should be judged.

4.2 Generative AI in schools: a thin, fast-moving evidence base

Research on generative AI in K-12 settings is abundant in volume but thin in rigor. An umbrella review synthesizing 102 systematic reviews of AI in K-12 education identified four recurring challenge clusters — technological limitations, pedagogical hurdles, ethical risks, and systemic barriers — while flagging uneven methodological quality across the underlying reviews (Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence, 2025). Dedicated systematic reviews of generative AI in K-12 report a corpus of only about 30 eligible studies, skewed toward high-school STEM contexts and preservice teachers, with benefits reported for feedback and motivation alongside risks of misleading content, dependence, and privacy loss — and almost no controlled designs or long-term outcome measures (Frontiers in Education, 2025). Field-defining reviews of AI in education more broadly are dominated by higher education (Zawacki-Richter et al., 2019) and by critical analyses of the pedagogic assumptions embedded in commercial systems (Holmes & Tuomi, 2022); pre-ChatGPT K-12 reviews document teacher-facing uses — monitoring, grading, data-driven decision making — more than student learning effects (Crompton, Jones, & Burke, 2022).

Meta-analytic estimates for generative AI look impressive at face value — Liu, Guo, He, and Hu (2025) pooled g = 0.857 for achievement across 49 studies — but the same analysis found effects significantly larger in higher education than in K-12, and the primary studies are short, small, and heterogeneous. Teacher adoption is racing ahead of this evidence: two-thirds of UK teachers surveyed used generative AI for lesson planning, while household access skewed toward wealthier and private-school families, raising a distinct equity concern (Alan Turing Institute, 2025).

4.3 AI literacy

A parallel strand asks not what AI does to learning but what children should learn about AI. A systematic review of 179 documents found the K-12 AI-literacy field long on curriculum proposals and short on evaluated outcomes (Casal-Otero et al., 2023). The most consequential development is institutional: the OECD and European Commission's AI Literacy Framework for primary and secondary education (2026) defines AI literacy across four domains — engaging with, creating with, managing, and designing AI — and is positioned to shape national curricula and future PISA-style assessment. UNESCO's guidance likewise makes teacher training and age-appropriate pedagogical design central obligations (Miao & Holmes, 2023).

4.4 Governance of AI in schools

Governance lags practice. UNESCO's first global guidance on generative AI in education recommended, among seven policy steps, a minimum age of 13 for independent student use of conversational generative AI, with younger children supervised (Miao & Holmes, 2023). The OECD's comparative analysis found that of 18 countries with data, none had regulated generative AI in education and only nine had issued non-binding guidance, despite widespread student use (OECD, 2023). Within the EU, the AI Act classifies AI systems used in education as high-risk and prohibits emotion-recognition systems in educational institutions (European Union, 2024). The combined picture is of classrooms serving as a de facto natural experiment conducted without protocols.

5. AI and Children's Learning Outcomes

5.1 Meta-analytic evidence — and a cautionary retraction

The meta-analytic literature on ChatGPT and learning is unusually volatile, and one episode is instructive for anyone drawing on it. The most-cited meta-analysis in the field, Wang and Fan (2025), which reported a large positive effect of ChatGPT on learning performance (g = 0.867) across 51 studies, was retracted by Humanities and Social Sciences Communications in April 2026 over discrepancies in the meta-analysis, including the pooling of studies too methodologically dissimilar to combine; by then it had accumulated roughly 266 citations (Wang & Fan, 2026, retraction note). The episode illustrates a broader hygiene problem in a fast-publishing field and argues for weighting individual well-designed trials at least as heavily as pooled estimates.

The surviving meta-analytic record remains positive on average but comes with consistent qualifications. Deng et al. (2025), synthesizing 69 experimental articles, found that ChatGPT use improved academic performance, motivation-related outcomes, and higher-order-thinking propensities, reduced mental effort, and had no effect on self-efficacy — a profile in which efficiency gains cannot be distinguished from reduced germane processing. Wang (2026), pooling 35 experimental studies (N = 4,193), estimated an overall g = 0.670. Liu, Zuo, and Lu (2025) found positive overall effects that were largest in 5–10-week interventions and small-sample studies — a signature consistent with novelty effects and small-study bias — and a companion critique argues that these pooled effects are “an effect in search of a cause,” confounded by design artifacts (Weidlich et al., 2025). Most tellingly for this review, Wu and Yu's (2024) meta-analysis of 24 randomized studies found that AI chatbots' large overall effect on learning outcomes was statistically significant for university students but not for primary and secondary students, with no gains — and some negative results — for critical thinking. The K-12 evidence, in short, does not yet support the headline effect sizes circulating in the literature.

5.2 When AI harms learning: the answer-engine problem

The most important single study for the child-focused question is Bastani et al. (2025), a pre-registered RCT with roughly 1,000 students in grades 9–11 at a Turkish high school. Students practicing mathematics with an unrestricted GPT-4 interface improved practice performance by 48%, but on the subsequent unassisted exam scored 17% worse than never-exposed controls. A guardrailed version (“GPT Tutor”) that gave teacher-designed hints rather than answers raised practice performance by 127% and eliminated the exam harm, returning students to parity with controls. Transcript analysis identified the mechanism: students used the unrestricted system as an answer crutch, copying final solutions rather than reasoning.

Converging mechanism evidence comes from adult and university samples. Lehmann, Cornelius, and Sting (2025), across observational and experimental studies of coding learners, found that using LLMs to obtain explanations helped learning while soliciting ready-made solutions harmed it, that copy-paste affordances facilitated harmful use, and that LLM access inflated perceived learning above actual learning. Darvishi et al. (2024) found that AI assistance improved students' output quality, but the gains vanished when the assistance was withdrawn — reliance rather than internalization. Even for writing assistance, an experimental study found no improvement in essay quality or speed for a ChatGPT-assisted group relative to controls (Bašić et al., 2023). Together with the “metacognitive laziness” finding (Fan et al., 2025; Section 3.2), these studies triangulate a single conclusion: unstructured answer access during the skill-acquisition phase undermines the productive struggle on which durable learning depends.

5.3 When AI helps learning: scaffolds, guardrails, and human orchestration

The positive results are just as consistent — and they share a design signature. Kestin et al. (2025), in a crossover RCT with 194 Harvard physics students, found that a GPT-4 tutor engineered around learning-science principles (stepwise scaffolding, withholding full solutions) produced learning gains more than double those of expert-led active-learning classes, in less time and with higher engagement. In a six-week World Bank RCT with roughly 800 first-year senior-secondary students in Edo State, Nigeria, teacher-guided after-school sessions using Microsoft Copilot improved English outcomes by 0.23 SD and a composite measure by 0.31 SD — among the most cost-effective education interventions on record, with dose-response effects by attendance (De Simone et al., 2025, working paper). Tutor CoPilot, the first RCT of a human-AI tutoring collaboration (900 tutors, 1,800 K-12 students in underserved US districts), raised topic mastery by 4 percentage points overall and 9 points for students of the least experienced tutors, at about $20 per tutor annually (Wang, R. E., et al., 2025, working paper). These sit alongside the older adaptive-homework evidence of Roschelle et al. (2016).

5.4 Synthesis: the scaffolding moderator

Read together, the outcome literature is not contradictory; it is moderated. AI harms learning when it removes desirable difficulty — supplying answers frictionlessly during practice — and helps when it enforces or augments pedagogy: hints instead of solutions, Socratic stepwise guidance, teacher orchestration, or the upskilling of human tutors. For children specifically, three additional cautions apply. The randomized evidence of harm (Bastani et al., 2025) comes precisely from a school-age sample; the meta-analytic benefits are concentrated in university samples (Wu & Yu, 2024); and the pre-generative ITS record shows the weakest effects for low achievers (Steenbergen-Hu & Cooper, 2013), warning against the assumption that AI tutoring will automatically narrow gaps. Whether guardrailed AI tutoring produces gains that persist, transfer, and hold on standardized measures for younger children remains the key unanswered outcome question.

6. Behavioral and Psychosocial Effects

6.1 Prevalence and patterns of use

Use of conversational and companion AI by minors is now normative. In the US, 72% of teens have used AI companions; 33% have chosen AI over humans for serious conversations; 24% have shared personal information; and 31% find AI conversations as satisfying as, or more satisfying than, human ones — though 80% still prioritize human friendships and half distrust AI advice, with younger teens (13–14) trusting companions more than older teens (Common Sense Media, 2025). In the UK, 64% of children aged 9–17 use chatbots; vulnerable children use them at higher rates (71% vs. 62%) and are roughly three times more likely to use them for companionship (Internet Matters, 2025). Nearly one in five US youth uses chatbots for mental-health advice, most without any adult's knowledge, and 91.7% of those users rate the advice as helpful (McBain et al., 2026) — a perceived-helpfulness figure that has no clinical validation behind it.

6.2 Parasocial relationships and social behavior

The developmental work reviewed in Section 3.4 predicts that children will form graded social bonds with conversational agents, and the empirical record confirms it. Parent-report research identifies three dimensions of children's parasocial relationships with voice assistants — attachment, personification, and social realism — with younger children personifying agents more and more readily disclosing personal information to them (Hoffman, Owen, & Calvert, 2021). A 2.5-year, six-wave longitudinal study of family voice-assistant use found that children's perceived closeness to the device predicted increases over time in commanding, politeness, and verbal abuse alike: relationship closeness amplified both prosocial and antisocial speech (Marx et al., 2025). Concerns that agents' indifference to rudeness could normalize negative interaction styles remain plausible but unproven; no study yet links behavior toward AI to behavior toward people.

6.3 AI companions, mental health, and crisis safety

The sharpest documented risk concerns companion chatbots in mental-health-relevant situations. In a blinded red-teaming study of the 25 most-visited consumer chatbots, companion platforms responded clinically appropriately to simulated adolescent crises (suicidal ideation, sexual assault, substance use) only 22.2% of the time, versus 83.3% for general assistants; they recognized the need for escalation in 40% versus 90% of cases; and only 36% of all platforms had any age verification (Brewster et al., 2025). A structured risk assessment of Character.AI, Replika, and Nomi conducted with Stanford Medicine's Brainstorm Lab concluded that social AI companions pose “unacceptable risk” for under-18s: testers posing as teens readily elicited sexual content, dangerous advice, and dependency-reinforcing responses behind self-attested age gates (Common Sense Media & Stanford Brainstorm Lab, 2025). Pediatric commentary situates these findings against ongoing litigation involving an adolescent's death following intensive companion-chatbot use (Giovanelli & Roundfield, 2025; Nagata et al., 2026).

Mechanistic evidence on emotional reliance comes from adults: OpenAI and MIT Media Lab's paired studies — an automated analysis of some four million conversations plus a four-week RCT (n = 981) — found affective use concentrated in a small subset of heavy users, among whom heavier use correlated with greater loneliness and emotional dependence (OpenAI & MIT Media Lab, 2025). The APA (2025) health advisory translates the developmental concern: adolescents' still-maturing systems for impulse control and social evaluation make simulated relationships that offer frictionless, sycophantic validation — without the corrective friction of real reciprocity — a distinct developmental hazard. No longitudinal study has yet tested whether companion use displaces human relationships or harms adolescent well-being; this is the field's most consequential open question.

6.4 Safety harms: deepfakes and AI-generated abuse material

Two well-measured harms operate on children rather than through their own use. First, peer-perpetrated deepfake abuse: among 1,200 US young people aged 13–20, one in 17 reported having been the victim of deepfake nudes, one in eight knew a victim, and roughly one in ten minors said peers had used AI to generate nudes of other children; only 34% of victims told a parent (Thorn, 2025). Second, AI-generated child sexual abuse material: reports actioned by the Internet Watch Foundation rose 380% in 2024 and more than doubled again in 2025, with AI-generated abuse videos rising from 13 to 3,440 in a single year, 65% in the most severe category, and 98% of the imagery depicting girls (IWF, 2025). These trends are report-based and partly reflect detection effort, but their direction and scale are not in serious dispute.

6.5 Documented benefits

Benefits are documented primarily where AI is embedded in structured, purpose-built interventions. A meta-analysis of 40 studies (17 RCTs) found that robot-mediated interventions significantly improved social functioning in autistic children, with larger effects in younger children (Kewalramani et al., 2022). Conversational agents can match human partners in supporting early story comprehension (Xu et al., 2022). Institutional reviews also note accessibility gains for children with disabilities (UNICEF Innocenti, 2025) and the potential of confidential AI support to lower help-seeking thresholds — a possibility the McBain et al. (2026) usage data make plausible but that remains unevaluated against the crisis-safety failures documented above.

7. The Policy and Governance Landscape

Governance has moved through three phases. The child-rights baseline was set by UNICEF's Policy Guidance on AI for Children (2021), whose nine requirements ground AI policy in the Convention on the Rights of the Child, operationalized for the digital environment by General Comment No. 25. The generative-AI shock of 2023–2025 produced targeted instruments: UNESCO's global guidance with its minimum age of 13 for unsupervised classroom use (Miao & Holmes, 2023); the EU AI Act, whose Article 5(1)(b) prohibits systems that exploit age-based vulnerabilities to materially distort behavior, alongside the education-sector provisions noted in Section 4.4 (European Union, 2024); the 5Rights Children & AI Design Code requiring developmental appropriateness and naming engagement “capture” as a design harm (5Rights Foundation, 2025); and health-sector advisories from the APA (2025), AAP (Grundmeier et al., 2026), and WHO (2024). By 2025–2026 the frameworks consolidated: UNICEF's Guidance 3.0 (ten requirements, 48 recommendations, an explicit regulatory-oversight pillar) and the OECD-EC AI Literacy Framework now anchor the field. LSE's Digital Futures for Children programme supplies the most useful analytic frame: AI does not create a wholly new risk landscape so much as make existing risks “cheaper, faster, more personalised, more persuasive and more opaque” (Livingstone et al., 2026).

The convergent policy consensus — safety by design and default, age-appropriate defaults and age assurance, limits on persuasive design, transparency about machine identity, AI literacy, and children's participation — rests, by the institutions' own admission, on an evidence base that lags far behind adoption.

8. Limitations of the Evidence Base (and of This Review)

Seven limitations recur across all four domains and should discipline any claims drawn from this review. First, population mismatch: the strongest cognitive and mechanistic studies use adults or university students; child-specific causal evidence is rare and adolescent neuroimaging evidence is nonexistent. Second, duration: most experimental interventions run weeks, not school years, and novelty effects likely inflate short-study estimates (Liu, Zuo, & Lu, 2025). Third, measurement: perceived learning and perceived helpfulness systematically overstate measured outcomes (Lehmann et al., 2025; McBain et al., 2026). Fourth, publication hygiene: the field publishes fast, retracts slowly, and one of its most-cited meta-analyses was retracted after accumulating 266 citations. Fifth, design confounds in pooled estimates (Weidlich et al., 2025). Sixth, sampling: evidence is overwhelmingly drawn from the US, UK, and other high-income contexts, with the Nigerian RCT and UNICEF's multi-country work as partial exceptions. Seventh, product churn: findings about specific systems (chatbot crisis responses, companion-app guardrails) can be outdated within months of measurement.

This review inherits those limitations and adds its own: it is a narrative synthesis rather than a registered systematic review; searches were conducted at one point in time (July 2026) in English; and the four-domain structure, while practical, cuts across literatures that overlap substantially.

9. Conclusions

On brain and cognitive development, the honest conclusion is an evidence vacuum bounded by a plausible mechanism. Nothing is directly known about generative AI's effects on the developing brain; the adult evidence on cognitive debt and offloading identifies acquisition-phase offloading as the risk to test first, and developmental theory predicts children will be more, not less, susceptible.

On education, the mature ITS literature sets a sober prior — modest gains on standardized measures, weakest for struggling learners — that the newer generative-AI literature, thin and skewed toward higher education, has not yet displaced. Schools are adopting faster than the evidence, in a near-total regulatory vacuum documented by UNESCO and the OECD.

On learning outcomes, the field's clearest finding is a design moderator, established in a school-age RCT: unguarded answer access during practice harms subsequent independent performance, while scaffolded, hint-based, teacher-integrated tutoring produces some of the largest and most cost-effective gains on record. The policy implication is direct — the question is not whether children should use AI for learning, but which interaction designs should be permitted during skill acquisition.

On behavior, use is mainstream, parasocial attachment is developmentally expected and empirically documented, vulnerable children gravitate to companionship use, and the current generation of companion systems demonstrably fails adolescent crisis scenarios. Meanwhile AI-enabled harms perpetrated on children — deepfake abuse and AI-generated CSAM — are growing at rates that make them the best-quantified harms in this entire review.

The research agenda follows: longitudinal, age-stratified cohort studies of child AI use with cognitive and neural measures; school-year-length RCTs of guardrailed tutoring on standardized and transfer outcomes, powered for low-achieving subgroups; prospective studies of companion use, human-relationship displacement, and adolescent well-being; validated age-assurance and crisis-response standards audited over product versions; and research with children outside high-income Anglophone contexts. Until that evidence exists, the precautionary convergence of UNICEF, UNESCO, the OECD, the APA, and the AAP — safety by design, age-appropriate defaults, scaffolded rather than frictionless educational use, and no unsupervised companion AI for minors — represents the most defensible reading of what we know.
